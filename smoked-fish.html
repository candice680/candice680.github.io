Smoked Fish (Xun Yu)

A delicate Shanghai delicacy featuring tender, flavorful fish with a distinctive smoky aroma

Introduction

Shanghai Smoked Fish (Xun Yu) is a refined delicacy featuring tender fish that has been carefully marinated, steamed, and then smoked to perfection. Unlike Western-style smoked fish, which often involves a long smoking process, the Shanghai method creates a more subtle smokiness that enhances rather than overwhelms the natural flavor of the fish. The result is a delicate balance of sweet, savory, and smoky notes, with a moist texture that melts in the mouth. Traditionally served cold as an appetizer, this dish exemplifies the nuanced flavors and meticulous preparation techniques characteristic of Shanghai cuisine.

Historical Background

The tradition of smoking fish in Shanghai dates back to the late Ming and early Qing dynasties (17th-18th centuries), when fresh fish from the region's abundant rivers and lakes was preserved using smoke from local tea leaves and sugar. This technique not only extended the shelf life of the fish but also imparted a unique flavor that became highly prized.

During the late 19th and early 20th centuries, as Shanghai developed into a major commercial hub, Xun Yu evolved from a preservation method into a refined culinary art. The dish became a staple in the city's teahouses and restaurants, particularly those catering to the emerging middle class and business elite. Each establishment would guard its unique smoking technique and marinade recipe, creating subtle variations throughout the city.

Traditionally, silver carp was the preferred fish for this preparation due to its delicate flesh and subtle flavor, though today various freshwater fish might be used. The authentic recipe always maintains the characteristic balance of sweetness from sugar, depth from soy sauce, and the distinctive aroma from a blend of tea leaves and spices used in the smoking process.

Preparation Method

  1. Select and prepare the fish: Choose a fresh, firm-fleshed white fish such as silver carp or grass carp. Clean thoroughly, remove scales, and cut into fillets about 1 inch thick. Pat dry with paper towels.
  2. Prepare the marinade: In a bowl, combine soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, sugar, white pepper, five-spice powder, and a pinch of salt. Mix until the sugar is completely dissolved.
  3. Marinate the fish: Place the fish fillets in a dish and pour the marinade over them, ensuring each piece is well-coated. Cover and refrigerate for at least 6 hours, preferably overnight, turning the fillets occasionally.
  4. Steam the fish: Remove the fish from the marinade (reserve the marinade) and arrange the pieces on a plate. Steam over medium-high heat for about 8-10 minutes until the fish is just cooked through but still moist.
  5. Prepare the smoking mixture: In a wok or deep pan, mix tea leaves (preferably Longjing or other green tea), brown sugar, star anise, and rice. Place a sheet of aluminum foil over this mixture.
  6. Create a smoking setup: Place a wire rack above the foil. Prick small holes in the foil to allow smoke to pass through. Heat the wok over medium-high heat until the mixture begins to smoke.
  7. Smoke the fish: Arrange the steamed fish fillets on the rack, ensuring they don't overlap. Cover tightly with a lid and reduce heat to medium-low. Smoke for about 10-15 minutes.
  8. Glaze the fish: While the fish is still warm, brush it with the reserved marinade that has been mixed with a small amount of honey.
  9. Cool and serve: Allow the fish to cool completely before serving. For the traditional presentation, slice the fish thinly on a diagonal and arrange decoratively on a plate, garnished with julienned scallions and ginger.

Tasting Notes

Shanghai Smoked Fish offers a complex sensory experience that begins with its inviting aroma—a delicate blend of sweet smokiness with hints of tea and spices. The fish itself should have a glistening, amber-colored exterior that promises the flavors to come.

The first bite reveals a perfectly moist texture—the fish should be tender and almost silky, never dry or tough. The flavor profile is multi-layered, beginning with the subtle sweetness from the sugar and honey glaze, followed by the umami richness from the soy sauce, and finishing with the aromatic notes from the smoking mixture.

What distinguishes Shanghai-style smoked fish from other smoked fish preparations is the perfect balance of these elements—no single flavor dominates, creating instead a harmonious blend that enhances the natural taste of the fish. The smoking process imparts just enough smoky character to add depth without overwhelming the delicate flavors of the marinade and the fish itself.

Traditionally served cold or at room temperature, the flavors are most pronounced when the dish is allowed to rest for a few hours after preparation, giving the various aromatics time to fully permeate the flesh.

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs fresh white fish fillets (silver carp or grass carp preferred)
  • For the marinade:
  • 3 tbsp light soy sauce
  • 2 tbsp dark soy sauce
  • 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine
  • 3 tbsp sugar
  • 1/2 tsp white pepper
  • 1/4 tsp five-spice powder
  • Pinch of salt
  • For the smoking mixture:
  • 1/4 cup tea leaves (Longjing or other green tea)
  • 2 tbsp brown sugar
  • 1 star anise
  • 1/4 cup uncooked rice
  • For finishing:
  • 1 tbsp honey
  • 2 scallions, julienned, for garnish
  • 1 inch ginger, julienned, for garnish

Cooking Information

Prep Time
30 mins
Marinate Time
6-12 hours
Cook Time
30 mins
Servings
6-8

Cooking Tips

Choose fish with firm, white flesh – avoid oily fish like mackerel or salmon, which would overpower the delicate smoking flavor.

Ensure your kitchen is well-ventilated when smoking the fish, or consider doing this step outdoors if possible.

The marinating time is crucial – too short, and the flavors won't penetrate; too long, and the salt may start to break down the delicate fish texture.

For a variation on the traditional recipe, you can add a small piece of cinnamon bark or a few Sichuan peppercorns to the smoking mixture for additional aromatic complexity.
