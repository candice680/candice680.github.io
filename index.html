History Historical Context

Shanghai cuisine, also known as "Benbang cuisine," can be traced back to the Ming and Qing dynasties. As one of China's largest port cities, Shanghai has been a hub where Eastern and Western cultures converge, and this convergence is reflected in its culinary traditions.

In the late 19th century, after Shanghai opened its port, it became an international trade center. Merchants from various regions brought their cooking techniques, creating a fusion of culinary styles. Shanghai cuisine absorbed the essence of Jiangsu and Zhejiang cuisines while incorporating cooking methods from Guangdong, Fujian, and even Western cuisine.

Shanghai dishes are known for their rich colors, mellow flavors, and exquisite craftsmanship. They emphasize the freshness of ingredients and skillfully use sugar and soy sauce for seasoning, creating the characteristic "thick oil and red sauce" style. Various cooking methods, from braising to steaming to stir-frying, have contributed to the diverse culinary landscape of Shanghai.

Crab Roe Buns Xie Huang Bao

Crab Roe Buns are one of Shanghai's most iconic dim sum dishes. Made with fresh crab meat and roe wrapped in a delicate steamed dough, these buns burst with rich, savory juices when bitten into. During crab season, traditional teahouses across Shanghai feature this mouth-watering delicacy on their menus.

Braised Pork Belly Hong Shao Rou

Shanghai-style Braised Pork Belly differs from other regions' versions in its unique seasoning proportions. With soy sauce and sugar as the main seasonings, the meat is tender but not greasy, with a glossy red appearance and melt-in-your-mouth texture. This dish carries the memories of Shanghai people, and from family kitchens to high-end restaurants, every chef has their secret recipe.

Xiaolongbao Soup Dumplings

Xiaolongbao is a quintessential part of Shanghai breakfast culture, characterized by its "thin skin, abundant filling, and rich soup." Traditional xiaolongbao requires specially prepared aspic that melts into a flavorful broth during steaming. When eating, one must carefully take a small bite, sip the soup, and then savor the filling. Nanxiang xiaolongbao is the most famous brand, with over a century of history.

About About This Site

The Shanghai Cuisine website is dedicated to documenting and preserving traditional Shanghai food culture, providing authentic local recipes for those who love cooking and Shanghai cuisine.

Shanghai cuisine has captivated countless food lovers with its unique flavors and exquisite cooking techniques. As the pace of modern life accelerates, many traditional culinary skills face the risk of being lost. Through this platform, we hope not only to record these precious recipes but also to pass on their cultural connotations and historical stories.

Each dish has a history and carries the memories and emotions of a specific era. We have documented the origin, evolution, characteristics, and traditional methods of each dish, hoping to help more people understand the charm of Shanghai cuisine.

We will continue to update the website content, add more Shanghai specialty dishes, and invite experienced local chefs to share their cooking secrets. Thank you for your interest in and support of Shanghai food culture!

— The Shanghai Cuisine Editorial Team
